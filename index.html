Star Wars: A Revised Cultural Phenomena

Click anywhere in the background to continue

A long time ago... In the 1970's, the film industry was dominated by movies that reflected the views of a cynical American people. The Vietnam War had left a sour taste, Nixon had left the populous questioning their leadership, and on top of that gas prices were up. Downbeat films with anti-heroes and questionable morals dominated the cinema, and in the midst of all the negativity, a fantastical coming of age story about a farmer, his wise old mentor, a scoundrel, and a princess broke onto the big screen and into people's hearts. Star Wars, a film that had struggled against all odds to get made, became an overnight sensation in the summer of 1977.

On top of being a classically fantastical film set in space, Star Wars won several Academy Awards for its unprecedented achievements and innovations in the film industry.

    Academy Awards won in 1979:

  • Best Art Direction
  • Best Costume Design
  • Best Sound
  • Best Film Editing
  • Best Effects, Visual Effects
  • Best Music, Original Score
  • Special Achievement Award for Ben Burtt

In 1989, the National Film Registry was opened as a section of the Library of Congress with the mission of preserving twenty-five culturally significant films every year for future generations. Star Wars (1977) was chosen as one of the first twenty-five films. However, to this day, the National Film Registry has yet to receive the copy of Star Wars that it asked for. Instead, LucasFilms sent a "Special Edition" copy of the film, that made arguably small but significant changes to the historical weight of the film. George Lucas and his former company simply refuse to release the theatrical release of the film, to either the National Film Registry, or the public.

The re-released "Special Edition" that was sent to the National Film Registry and is circulated around the public has admittedly brushed up the film in certain respects. It has been cleaned up in high definition, with clearer colors and sounds. However, there are numerous needless changes that detract from the original film, and oftentimes take away many of the aspects that had made the film culturally significant and win its Academy Awards. From changing original aliens into already-dated computer generated ones to revising certain shots, the "Special Edition" tampers with the magic of Star Wars.

Much of the award-winning costume design has been replaced by computer- generated aliens. Not only do these aliens already look dated by modern standards, they also look out of place in this film from 1977 and overwrite one of the aspects that had won the film an Academy Award.

Some of the award-winning sound effects were changed with the "Special Edition" for no reason at all.

In the famous case of "Han Shot First!" a few frames were added in that undermine both the nuanced character of Han Solo, and the award-winning editing.

The original visual effects in Star Wars were unprecedented and had paved a whole new path within the industry for story-telling. However, like many other award-winning aspects of the theatrical release, this one was replaced in many places by computer generated effects in the "Special Edition."

George Lucas made a bold and historical move in 1977 by re-establishing classical music into cinema. John Williams' sweeping score can be remembered on the spot by millions of people worldwide. Yet, once again, in Lucas' continuing frenzy to tinker with his movie, he drowns out Williams' scores with new sound effects in certain parts of the movie, further corroding the original masterpiece. (Click the YouTube button and watch between 4:14 and 4:30)

“To me, the Special Edition ones are the films I wanted to make, Anybody that makes films knows the film is never finished. It’s abandoned or it’s ripped out of your hands, and it’s thrown into the marketplace, never finished." - George Lucas on the Special Editions

George Lucas claims the "Special Edition" is simply how he would have done Star Wars if the technology was there at the time. Although Lucas has a point here, the question becomes whether Star Wars, a landmark of American cinema and worldwide phenomenon, still belongs to him and his company, or to the people that adore it, and whether he has the right to continuously tinker with and re-release an adored film.

“ People who alter or destroy works of art and our cultural heritage for profit or as an exercise of power are barbarians, and if the laws of the United States continue to condone this behavior, history will surely classify us as a barbaric society,” George Lucas on the remastering of classic films.

Anyone that has worked on any assignment feels it could continue to be improved upon, however, in the case of Star Wars, the original theatrical release was historical, and its preservation is key to both American cinema's history and culture, and to its loyal fans. A long time ago, George Lucas may have agreed with this point as well.

By changing the film, George Lucas is tampering with history itself. Personally, I did not even know that editions had been made to the movie the first time I watched it. If someone mentioned the historical significance of the effects, I naturally assumed the effects that were mentioned were the ones I had seen in the movie. Little did I know that so many of the historical, award-winning aspects of the original movie had been changed before I had seen it. This is a problem when entire generations of viewers do not know what was in the original film and what was not, and therefore cannot connect with the past and the historical and cultural significance of the film. For all these reasons and more, LucasFilm should release the original theatrical release of the film to both the National Film Registry and the public, so that this landmark in cinematic history may be preserved for future generations to come.
